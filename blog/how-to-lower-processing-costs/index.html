1

Negotiate Your Processor's Markup

Many business owners don't realize that the processor's markup is negotiable. While interchange fees (paid to the card networks) are fixed, the amount your processor charges on top is entirely up for negotiation.

If you've been with your processor for a while, have good volume, or are considering switching, you have leverage. Call and ask for a rate review.

Potential savings: Reducing your markup from 0.5% to 0.3% on $100,000/month in sales saves $200/month or $2,400/year.
2

Switch to Interchange-Plus Pricing

If you're on flat-rate pricing (like Square's 2.6% + $0.10), you're likely overpaying. Interchange-plus pricing passes through the actual interchange cost plus a transparent markup.

Since most debit cards and basic credit cards have interchange rates around 1.5-1.8%, you'll save significantly compared to paying 2.6% on everything.

Potential savings: A business processing 70% debit cards could save 0.5-1% on most transactions.
3

Eliminate Unnecessary Fees

Review your statement for fees you may not need:

  • PCI compliance fee: Often $50-100/year â€” negotiate removal
  • Statement fee: $5-15/month for paper statements â€” go paperless
  • Batch fee: $0.10-0.25 per batch â€” some processors waive this
  • Monthly minimum: Can sometimes be removed
Potential savings: Eliminating unnecessary fees can save $50-150/month.
4

Settle Batches Daily

Transactions that settle within 24 hours of authorization typically qualify for lower interchange rates. If you're not settling daily, you could be paying higher "non-qualified" rates.

Set your terminal to auto-batch every night, ideally before midnight.

5

Use Address Verification (AVS)

For keyed-in and online transactions, using AVS (Address Verification Service) can qualify your transactions for lower interchange rates. It also reduces fraud risk.

Ensure your payment system is set to collect and verify billing addresses.

6

Encourage Debit Card Usage

Debit cards typically have lower interchange fees than credit cards. Consider subtle encouragements:

  • Train staff to ask "Debit or credit?" when appropriate
  • Consider offering small discounts for debit
7

Set Minimum Purchase Amounts

The Durbin Amendment allows merchants to set minimum purchase amounts up to $10 for credit cards. On small transactions, the per-transaction fee can eat up a significant percentage of the sale.

Setting a $5-10 minimum for credit cards is legal and common.

8

Review Statements Monthly

Keep historical records to track trends over time.

9

Get Competitive Quotes Annually

Even if you're happy with your processor, get competing quotes once a year. This gives you leverage to negotiate and ensures you're still getting a competitive deal.

Share quotes with your current processor â€” they'll often match or beat them to keep your business.

10

Avoid Long-Term Contracts

Long-term contracts with early termination fees lock you in even if you find better rates. Look for month-to-month agreements that let you switch without penalty.

If you're in a contract, know when it expires and start shopping before renewal.

11

Consider Cash Discounting

Cash discounting programs offer customers a discount for paying with cash, effectively passing processing costs to card users. This is legal and becoming increasingly common.

Note: This is different from illegal surcharging. Consult with your processor about compliant programs.
