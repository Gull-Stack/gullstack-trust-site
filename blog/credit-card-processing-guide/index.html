What is Credit Card Processing?

Credit card processing is the system that allows businesses to accept payments from credit and debit cards. When a customer swipes, taps, or enters their card information, a complex network of financial institutions work together to transfer funds from the customer's account to yours.

The key players in credit card processing include:

  • Merchant (you) â€” The business accepting the payment
  • Customer (cardholder) â€” The person making the purchase
  • Payment processor â€” The company handling the transaction
  • Card network â€” Visa, Mastercard, Amex, Discover
  • Issuing bank â€” The customer's bank that issued the card
  • Acquiring bank â€” The bank that holds your merchant account

How Credit Card Processing Works

Here's what happens in the seconds between when a customer swipes their card and when you see "Approved":

1. Authorization (1-2 seconds)

When the card is swiped, the payment terminal sends the card data through your processor to the card network, which routes it to the issuing bank. The bank checks if the card is valid and has sufficient funds, then sends back an approval or decline.

2. Batching (End of day)

Throughout the day, approved transactions are stored in a "batch." At the end of the day (usually automatically), you "close" the batch and send all transactions for settlement.

3. Settlement (1-2 business days)

The card networks and banks transfer the funds. Money moves from the issuing bank to the acquiring bank, minus the various fees. Then it's deposited into your business bank account.

Types of Credit Card Processing Fees

Understanding fees is crucial because they directly impact your bottom line. Here are the main fee types:

Interchange Fees

These are set by the card networks (Visa, Mastercard) and paid to the issuing bank. They're non-negotiable and typically range from 1.5% to 3.5% depending on the card type.

Assessment Fees

Small fees charged by the card networks themselves. Usually around 0.13% to 0.15% of the transaction.

Processor Markup

This is where your payment processor makes their money. It's the only truly negotiable part of your rate. Good processors charge 0.2% to 0.5% on top of interchange.

Other Potential Fees

  • Monthly fees: $10-50/month for account maintenance
  • PCI compliance fees: $50-100/year for security compliance
  • Chargeback fees: $15-25 per disputed transaction
  • Equipment fees: $0-50/month for terminal rental
  • Early termination fees: $200-500+ if you cancel early

Credit Card Processing Pricing Models

Processors use different pricing models to charge for their services. Understanding these is key to comparing providers:

Flat-Rate Pricing

You pay the same rate on every transaction regardless of card type. Companies like Square and Stripe use this model (e.g., 2.6% + $0.10 per transaction).

Best for: Low-volume businesses, simple pricing preferences

Interchange-Plus Pricing

You pay the actual interchange fee plus a fixed markup from your processor (e.g., interchange + 0.3% + $0.10).

Best for: Higher-volume businesses, transparent pricing

Tiered Pricing

Transactions are grouped into tiers (qualified, mid-qualified, non-qualified) with different rates. Often the least transparent option.

Best for: Avoid if possible â€” usually the most expensive

How to Choose a Payment Processor

With hundreds of processors competing for your business, here's what to look for:

1. Transparent Pricing

Avoid processors that won't clearly explain their fees. Ask for interchange-plus pricing and get everything in writing.

2. No Long-Term Contracts

Month-to-month agreements protect you. Be wary of 3-year contracts with early termination fees.

3. Good Customer Support

When your terminal goes down on a busy Saturday, you need help fast. Look for 24/7 support with real humans.

4. Modern Technology

Make sure they support EMV chip cards, contactless payments (Apple Pay, Google Pay), and integrate with your POS system.

5. Fast Deposits

Same-day or next-day funding helps your cash flow. Don't settle for 3-5 day deposit times.

6. Added Value

Some processors offer extra services like marketing, website hosting, or business analytics. These can add significant value beyond just payment processing.

Setting Up Credit Card Processing

Ready to start accepting cards? Here's the process:

Step 1: Gather Your Documents

  • Business license or registration
  • EIN or Social Security Number
  • Bank account information
  • Voided check or bank letter
  • Recent processing statements (if switching)

Step 2: Apply for a Merchant Account

Submit your application online or through a sales rep. Approval typically takes 1-3 business days for most businesses.

Step 3: Get Your Equipment

Choose between a countertop terminal, mobile reader, or integrated POS system based on your business needs.

Step 4: Set Up and Test

Install your equipment, run test transactions, and train your staff before going live.
